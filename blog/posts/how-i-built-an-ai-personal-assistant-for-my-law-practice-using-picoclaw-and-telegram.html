How I Built an AI Personal Assistant for My Law Practice Using PicoClaw and Telegram

Practical lessons from running a Nigerian law practice with an open-source AI assistant on Telegram

---

The Problem

Every lawyer knows the struggle: multiple cases, scattered documents, endless deadlines, clients to onboard, research to do, invoices to send. You're juggling WhatsApp messages, email threads, physical files, and maybe a Google Drive folder or two. Something always falls through the cracks.

I tried various solutions — practice management software, calendar apps, to-do lists — but none of them fit how I actually work. Most are either too rigid (forcing me into their workflow) or require me to sit at a desk to use them.

What I needed was something that lived where I already am: my phone.

Enter PicoClaw 🦞

PicoClaw is an ultra-lightweight AI assistant written in Go. It's open-source, runs on a Linux server (or even a Raspberry Pi), and connects to Telegram as a chat interface. It's designed to be practical, fast, and extensible through "skills" — modular capabilities you can add or build yourself.

Here's what makes it different from off-the-shelf legal tech:

  • **It's a conversation, not a dashboard.** I talk to it like I talk to a junior associate.
  • **It's modular.** I built exactly the tools I need, nothing I don't.
  • **It's always in my pocket.** Telegram runs on every phone.
  • **It's private.** My data stays on my own server.

Installation: Surprisingly Simple

The setup is straightforward if you have basic Linux familiarity:

# Download the binary
wget https://github.com/vitozorin/picoclaw/releases/latest/download/picoclaw-linux-amd64.tar.gz
tar -xzf picoclaw-linux-amd64.tar.gz

# Create workspace
mkdir -p ~/.picoclaw/workspace

# Configure
# Edit config.yaml with your Telegram bot token and settings

The Telegram integration works through a bot. You create one via @BotFather on Telegram, get your API token, and point PicoClaw to it. That's it — you now have an AI assistant in your Telegram.

The server runs as a systemd service, so it starts on boot and stays running:

[Unit]
Description=PicoClaw AI Assistant
After=network.target

[Service]
ExecStart=/home/user/picoclaw
WorkingDirectory=/home/user/.picoclaw
Restart=always
User=user

[Install]
WantedBy=multi-user.target

The Skills That Matter for a Lawyer

PicoClaw's power comes from its skills. Here are the ones I built and use daily:

1. Case Manager — Never Miss a Deadline

This is the backbone of my practice. It tracks every case with:

  • **Milestones** — filing dates, hearings, judgment dates
  • **Checklists** — documents needed, steps remaining
  • **Deadline alerts** — automatic reminders sent to my Telegram

For example, with Maduemem v. Osamwoyi (MCA/507/CIV/2026) — a landlord/tenant matter at Apapa Magistrate Court — I know at a glance: judgment is fixed for 24 August 2026, the motion to regularise defence needs filing by 1 July, and I have a prep session on 29 June. All of this is one command away.

# What's my dashboard look like?
> python3 skills/case-manager/scripts/case-tracker.py dashboard

2. MaiGai Legal Research — My Law Librarian

MaiGai is a specialized legal research agent focused on maritime law, international trade, and petroleum downstream law — my primary practice areas. It searches a library of 64+ documents (statutes, cases, law review articles) using RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation).

When I needed to build a case against the Nigeria Customs Service over their e-auction portal, MaiGai found Section 119 of the NCS Act 2023 — the provision requiring court orders for disposal of goods. It also surfaced a Punch newspaper investigation about "Direct Auction Allocation" letters that bypass the e-auction system entirely. That kind of targeted research would have taken hours manually.

> python3 skills/maigai-legal-research/scripts/maigai.py research "NCS e-auction legal framework"

3. Document Drafter — Court-Ready Filings

Legal drafting in Nigeria has specific formatting requirements — the headings, the fonts, the citation styles. The Document Drafter skill handles all of that, producing motions, legal opinions, engagement letters, and correspondence in proper Nigerian court format.

It pulls citations directly from the RAG library, so when I cite a statute, it's accurate and sourced. No more flipping through statute books to verify section numbers.

4. Client Onboarding — From Conversation to Client in Minutes

This is one of my favourites. When a prospective client reaches out, I have a natural conversation with them. Afterward, I feed my discussion notes to the onboarding pipeline, and it:

  1. Extracts structured data from the notes
  2. Generates a pre-filled onboarding form
  3. Creates an engagement letter
  4. Sets up vault files in my Obsidian knowledge base
  5. Creates a Google Drive folder with proper permissions
  6. Generates an invoice
  7. Adds calendar events for follow-ups

    8. When I onboarded LRIM / Mr. Mufutau Abayomi Longe — a retainer client for legal advisory services at the Lekki Free Trade Zone — the entire process took minutes. The retainer invoice (₦1,200,000 for six months) was generated, the Google Doc for the feasibility study was created and shared, and the onboarding email was sent — all from my phone.

    5. Payment & Invoice — Get Paid Faster

    Invoicing is automated. I can generate a professional HTML invoice, track payment status, and get a dashboard view of outstanding payments. The format follows my firm's conventions (VO/{CLIENT_CODE}/{YEAR}/{SEQUENCE}), and it includes all the necessary details: bank account information, breakdown of fees, and payment terms.

    6. Google Drive Integration — Files Where I Need Them

    Every case gets a Google Drive folder with consistent structure. Documents are uploaded, shared with the right permissions, and organised. When I need a case brief or a cross-examination plan, it's one command away — no digging through Drive's search.

    7. Ushe — My Personal Coordinator

    Ushe is the orchestrator. It manages my calendar, delegates tasks to other agents (MaiGai for research, Sewa for content), and generates daily briefings.

    Every morning, I get a briefing that tells me:

    • What's happening today (court dates, meetings, deadlines)
    • What tasks are pending
    • What's coming up this week

    Every evening, I get a summary of what was accomplished and what needs attention tomorrow.

    8. Sewa — Content Creator

    Sewa handles blog posts, social media content, and marketing assets. When I want to publish an article on the firm's website (lexvita.pages.dev), Sewa drafts it, suggests a featured image, and I publish it with a single command. The site publisher auto-commits to GitHub, and Cloudflare Pages deploys it automatically.

    A Day in the Life

    Here's what my typical day looks like with PicoClaw:

    7:00 AM — Morning briefing arrives on Telegram. I see today's prep session for Maduemem v. Osamwoyi, a reminder that the motion filing deadline is in 2 days, and a note that the Customs E-Auction daily tracking log was recorded.

    9:00 AM — At chambers, I review the cross-examination questions MaiGai prepared. I notice a critical finding: the wrong law was cited in the claimant's case (Apapa is exempt from the Tenancy Law 2011). I ask PicoClaw to save this to the case file.

    12:00 PM — A potential client calls. After the conversation, I dictate notes to PicoClaw and say "onboard this." By the time I hang up, the engagement letter is ready.

    3:00 PM — I check the Customs E-Auction portal. It's still down (DNS resolution failure — Day 3 of tracking). PicoClaw logs this automatically.

    6:00 PM — Evening briefing. I review what was accomplished and what's pending for tomorrow.

    All of this happens through Telegram. I never open a dashboard. I never log into a practice management system. It's just conversation.

    The Tech Stack

    For those interested in the technical side:

    • **PicoClaw** — Go binary, ~15MB, runs on a Linux VPS
    • **Telegram Bot API** — Chat interface
    • **Python scripts** — Most skills are Python, called by PicoClaw
    • **Obsidian vault** — Knowledge base and document storage
    • **Google Drive API** — File storage and sharing
    • **Google Apps Script** — Onboarding form backend
    • **Git + GitHub** — Version control and site deployment
    • **Cloudflare Pages** — Website hosting
    • **RAG index** — Local vector search for legal documents

    The whole thing runs on modest hardware. No GPU needed. No expensive API subscriptions (beyond the LLM API itself).

    What I've Learned

    1. **Start with pain points, not technology.** I built skills as I needed them, not in advance. The case manager came first because I missed a deadline once. The onboarding pipeline came after I spent an hour manually setting up a client file.
      1. **Conversation is the best interface.** I've tried dozens of legal tech products with complex UIs. Nothing beats being able to say "what's happening with the Osamwoyi case?" and getting an answer.
        1. **Your data, your control.** Everything runs on my server. No third-party legal tech company has access to my case files, client information, or strategy notes.
          1. **Build for mobile-first.** If I can't use it from my phone, I won't use it consistently. Telegram on my phone is where I already spend time.
            1. **Automate the boring stuff.** Client onboarding, invoice generation, document formatting, daily tracking logs — these are mechanical tasks that a computer does better than a human. Let the AI handle them.

              2. Getting Started

              If you're a lawyer interested in building something similar:

              1. Set up PicoClaw on a Linux server (DigitalOcean droplet starts at $6/month)
              2. Create a Telegram bot via @BotFather
              3. Start with one skill — the one that solves your biggest pain point
              4. Add skills gradually as you identify needs
              5. Build your knowledge base over time (every case file, every statute, every template)

                6. The code is open-source and free. The skills I've built are available in the workspace templates. You don't need to be a programmer — if you can describe what you need in plain English, an AI assistant can help you build it.
